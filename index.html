002 — The Problem

What's really in your activewear?

A 2024 study from the University of Birmingham found that nanoplastics from synthetic textiles can penetrate human skin cells and accumulate in tissue. Every time you pull on a polyester legging, you're wrapping your body in plastic.

During exercise, your pores open. You sweat. Your skin becomes more permeable. And those microscopic plastic particles? They don't just sit on the surface — they migrate inward.

The activewear industry has normalized synthetic fabrics as "performance" materials. We're here to challenge that narrative with science, transparency, and a radically better alternative.

006 — The Science

Research-backed materials.

Research from the University of Birmingham (2024) demonstrated that polystyrene nanoplastics as small as 100nm can penetrate human skin cells in vitro. During exercise, increased skin permeability through sweating and pore dilation may accelerate this absorption.

Source: University of Birmingham, Environment International, 2024

A single polyester garment can release up to 700,000 microplastic fibers in one domestic wash cycle. These fibers pass through wastewater treatment and enter waterways, eventually contaminating marine ecosystems and re-entering the food chain.

Source: Plymouth University, Marine Pollution Bulletin, 2016

TENCEL™ Lyocell fibers demonstrate 50% greater moisture absorption compared to cotton, with superior moisture vapor transmission rates. The closed-loop production process recovers 99%+ of solvents, making it one of the most environmentally responsible fiber production methods available.

Source: Lenzing AG Technical Documentation

Supima® cotton features extra-long staple (ELS) fibers averaging 35mm+ in length — nearly twice the length of standard upland cotton. This results in a fiber that is 45% stronger, significantly softer, and more resistant to pilling, with superior color retention after repeated washing.

Source: Supima Association, supima.com

The Global Organic Textile Standard (GOTS) is the world's leading processing standard for organic fibers. It covers the entire textile supply chain with strict environmental criteria (wastewater treatment, chemical restrictions, energy use) and social criteria (fair wages, safe working conditions, no child labor).

Source: Global Organic Textile Standard, global-standard.org
